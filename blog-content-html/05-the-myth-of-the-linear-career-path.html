The Myth of the Linear Career Path: Why "Up" Isn't the Only Way Forward

Category: Career Development
Reading Time: 6 minutes

The Linear Career Myth: Where It Came From

The "career ladder" made sense in the industrial era:
- Jobs were stable
- Industries were predictable
- Loyalty was rewarded
- One company, one climb

But that world no longer exists.

Today:
- The average person changes careers 5-7 times
- Entire industries emerge and disappear within decades
- Skills have shorter shelf lives
- Companies restructure constantly

Yet we're still using a 20th-century career model in a 21st-century reality.

Why "Up" Isn't Always the Answer

Promotion Isn't Always Progress

Not everyone wants to manage people.
Not everyone thrives in senior leadership.
Not everyone defines success as title and salary.

Questions to ask before pursuing promotion:
- Do I want the actual responsibilities, or just the status?
- Will this role use my strengths or require me to develop new ones I'm less interested in?
- Am I chasing someone else's definition of success?

Lateral Moves Build Broader Skills

Staying in one function limits your perspective. Moving laterally:
- Builds diverse skill sets
- Expands your network
- Increases adaptability
- Opens unexpected opportunities

Example: A finance professional who moves into operations gains cross-functional insight that makes them a stronger future CFO than someone who stayed in finance their whole career.

Strategic "Pauses" Aren't Wasted Time

Career breaks, sabbaticals, and transitions create space for:
- Skill development
- Reflection and recalibration
- Caring for family or health
- Exploring new interests

The myth says: gaps in your CV are red flags.
The reality: intentional pauses often lead to better long-term decisions.

Redefining Career Success

Instead of asking "Am I moving up?" ask:

1. Am I Learning?

Growth matters more than titles. Are you developing skills that will serve you long-term?

2. Am I Energised?

Does your work give you energy or drain it? Sustainability matters.

3. Am I Aligned?

Does this role reflect your values, strengths, and interests? Or are you chasing external validation?

4. Am I Building Optionality?

Are you creating opportunities for future moves, or boxing yourself in?

How to Navigate a Non-Linear Career

1. Embrace Exploration

Give yourself permission to try things.

  • Volunteer in areas you're curious about
  • Take on stretch projects outside your core role
  • Experiment through side projects before committing fully

Exploration isn't distractionâ€”it's strategic research.

2. Build Transferable Skills

Invest in skills that travel across industries:
- Communication and storytelling
- Problem-solving and critical thinking
- Relationship-building and influence
- Adaptability and learning agility

These matter more than technical expertise tied to one field.

3. Rewrite Your Narrative

When explaining your non-linear path:

Don't say: "I've jumped around a lot."
Do say: "I've intentionally built diverse experience in [X, Y, Z], which gives me a unique perspective on [outcome]."

Example:
"I've worked across education, technology, and consulting, which allows me to design learning experiences that are both pedagogically sound and technically feasible."

4. Curate Your Professional Identity

Your CV doesn't have to be chronological. Consider:
- Functional CV: Organised by skill areas, not timeline
- Narrative CV: Tells a story of intentional growth
- Portfolio approach: Show outcomes, not just roles

5. Find Your "Red Thread"

Even in non-linear careers, there's often a consistent theme.

Ask: "What's the through-line in my work?"

Examples:
- Helping organisations scale
- Making complex things accessible
- Building communities
- Solving problems creatively

Your red thread is what connects seemingly disparate experiences into a coherent story.

When to Go Linear vs. Non-Linear

Linear Makes Sense When:

  • You're in a field with clear progression (medicine, law, academia)
  • You're deeply passionate about a specific craft and want mastery
  • You thrive with structure and predictable growth
  • You're in an industry that rewards tenure

Non-Linear Makes Sense When:

  • You have multiple interests and don't want to pick just one
  • You value learning over titles
  • You're in fast-changing industries (tech, media, social impact)
  • You want flexibility and autonomy

Neither is better. But most people assume linear is the defaultâ€”and it's not.
